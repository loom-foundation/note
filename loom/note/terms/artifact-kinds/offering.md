The value a product or service offers a persona, captured as a profile over its [Pain Reliever](pain-reliever.md){id=note:term:7qrdxnp} and [Gain Creator](gain-creator.md){id=note:term:g9fxrs5} facet artifacts: the reliefs it offers for pains and the gains it creates, said in the persona's own terms.

Each pain-reliever and gain-creator is a first-class artifact that declares itself part of the Offering; the Offering states value, not an obligation on the system and not the persona's problem.

An Offering answers a Need; the correspondence between its relievers and creators and the Need's pains and gains is the fit, a computed value proposition.

## Inclusion test

Does it state, in the customer's terms, the reliefs and gains a product or service offers in answer to a persona's pains and gains?

If it states what must be true of the system, it is a Requirement; if it states the persona's problem without offering value, it is a Need; if it is the customer-facing pitch of that value, it is a Value Proposition; if it states what the organization stands for rather than the value it offers, it is positioning, which belongs to the strategy and brand layer, not here.

## Relations

- *addresses* [Need](need.md){id=note:term:x4vqdj1}
- *contrasts with* [Requirement](requirement.md){id=note:term:tr9zna6} (offered value versus obligation)

## Origin

General English (Oxford: an offering is "a thing offered"), narrowed here to the value a product or service offers a customer.

It is the value map of the Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder, Pigneur, Bernarda, and Smith, *Value Proposition Design*, 2014, CC BY-SA 3.0), set against a customer profile (a persona's jobs, pains, and gains), with the fit between them the thing to get right.

The Canvas calls this side the "value map"; this corpus names it the Offering, after marketing's market offering (Kotler), because "map" reads as the fit between the two sides rather than the offered value itself.
