The authored value-claim for an [Offering](offering.md){id=note:term:n5vn3ke}: the customer-facing pitch that states, in the persona's terms, the value the Offering delivers in answer to a Need.

It is the single statement product copy, marketing, and sales restate, distinct from the Offering (the structured value) and from the fit (the computed correspondence that substantiates it).

A Value Proposition is optional: by default the value proposition is the computed fit, and a statement is authored only when the pitch is worth capturing as a reusable claim.

## Inclusion test

Is it the customer-facing claim, the pitch, of an Offering's value, rather than the structured relievers and creators themselves (an Offering) or the computed correspondence to a Need's pains and gains (the fit)?

If it states what must be true of the system, it is a Requirement; if it states the persona's problem, it is a Need.

## Relations

- *expresses* [Offering](offering.md){id=note:term:n5vn3ke}

## Origin

The term of art was coined by Lanning and Michaels at McKinsey in 1988 ("A business is a value delivery system"), who defined it as a clear statement of the benefits a firm provides a customer segment.

"Benefit" in that founding sense, and in benefit segmentation (Haley, 1968) and means-end chain theory (Gutman, 1982), is a customer-sought outcome, not the offered side, which this corpus names a pain-reliever or gain-creator.

The Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder et al., 2014) treats the fit between a value map and a customer profile as the value proposition; this corpus reserves the term for the authored statement of that fit, the pitch, and computes the fit itself.
