An agreed, all-or-nothing coordination rule for the making of artifacts: a practice binding because it was agreed and is uniformly applied, whose specific content is arbitrary among workable alternatives, the value living in the uniformity itself.

A Convention may name the mechanism, format, or style it fixes, because the agreed mechanism is its substance; it binds by agreement, not by obligating the System.

## Inclusion test

Is it binding because agreed rather than because intrinsically right, followed or violated per instance, with alternatives that would have served equally well had they been agreed instead?

If it is weighed against other commitments rather than applied all-or-nothing, it is a Principle; if it obligates the System and must stay solution-free, it is a Requirement; if an external force imposes the mechanism, it is a constraint-typed Requirement; if it commits the design that satisfies a requirement, it is a Specification.

## Relations

- *contrasts with* [Principle](principle.md){id=note:term:q2snn7e} (weighed and balanced versus applied all-or-nothing; intrinsic content versus agreed content)
- *contrasts with* [Requirement](requirement.md){id=note:term:tr9zna6} (an agreed practice on the making versus a solution-free obligation on the System)
- *relates to* [Decision Record](decision-record.md){id=note:term:met4a44} (born from, where the choice among alternatives was weighed; a lightweight convention may rest on review alone)

## Origin

General English (Oxford: "a way in which something is usually done, especially within a particular area or activity").

The philosophical grounding is Lewis (*Convention: A Philosophical Study*, 1969): a convention is an arbitrary but self-sustaining regularity that solves a recurring coordination problem, conformed to because everyone else conforms; driving on one agreed side of the road is the canonical case.

In software practice the sense is carried by coding conventions, style guides, and commit conventions, where the agreed shape, not its particular content, is what pays.

Set against the Dworkin distinction the corpus uses for Principle: a Convention applies in the all-or-nothing fashion of his rules, where a Principle has weight and is balanced; and unlike either, its content is arbitrary, so changing a Convention by re-agreement loses nothing, provided everyone changes together.
