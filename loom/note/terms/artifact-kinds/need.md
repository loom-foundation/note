A problem-space profile of a persona's problem, captured as a profile over its [Job](job.md){id=note:term:41qzg6f}, [Pain](pain.md){id=note:term:hb74nmn}, and [Gain](gain.md){id=note:term:7e4f92v} facet artifacts, before any obligation is stated.

Each job, pain, and gain is a first-class artifact that declares itself part of the Need; the Need carries the persona and an optional validation, and no longer holds those facets inline.

A Need states a problem, never a solution.

## Inclusion test

Does it describe a problem a persona has, as a profile of what they are trying to do and what hurts, without naming a solution?

If it names a solution, it is not a Need; the solution-bearing part is a Requirement or a Specification, or, where it is neither, a defect.

If a facet names what the product does or offers, rather than what the persona suffers or wants, it is a pain-reliever or gain-creator on an Offering, not a Pain or Gain on a Need.

A Need, and each of its facets, is stated in the persona's own terms and stays on the problem: it may name the kind of thing the persona needs in their domain, but it does not obligate the system ("shall…", a Requirement) or prescribe a specific mechanism or standard (a leaked solution); what the product offers in answer belongs to an Offering.

## Relations

- *carries* [Validation](../validation/validation.md){id=note:term:13esbh9}
- *contrasts with* [Requirement](requirement.md){id=note:term:tr9zna6} (problem versus obligation)
- *contrasts with* [Offering](offering.md){id=note:term:n5vn3ke} (problem versus offered value)

## Origin

General English (Oxford: "a thing that is wanted or required").

Sharpened in the jobs-to-be-done tradition: the marketing adage that a customer wants the hole, not the drill (popularized by Levitt); Ulwick's outcome-driven innovation, which frames needs as stable, solution-free desired outcomes; and Christensen's "job to be done", with the job-story form (Klement) casting a need as a situation, a motivation, and a desired outcome rather than a feature request.

Its jobs, pains, and gains structure follows the customer profile of the Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder et al.), itself heir to the voice-of-the-customer tradition that translated customer needs into design in quality function deployment (Akao, late 1960s).
