Every artifact, section, table, field, or convention must carry distinct, load-bearing substance.
The corpus does not introduce things that duplicate content canonicalized elsewhere or add ceremony without proportionate value.

New artifacts must earn their place; existing artifacts are absorbed or deleted when their substance migrates.

## Why this matters

A corpus that costs more to maintain than it returns collapses under its own weight.

Every new artifact is a new place for drift; every new field is one more thing to reconcile.

The bar for adding is higher than the bar for not adding.
When in doubt, do not create.

A second-order cost: ceremony without substance erodes trust.
Once a reader notices that some sections restate others, the reader begins to discount everything.

The risks this principle mitigates are maintenance collapse, when the corpus costs more to keep than it returns, and the trust debt that accrues when ceremony without substance teaches readers to discount the whole.

## How to apply

Before authoring a new document, section, field, or column, ask two questions.
Does this carry substance that no other location carries?
Would removing it lose information?
If the answer to either is no, do not add it.

When reviewing, the first check is whether each section earns its place: sections that restate other documents are rejected, not edited; sections that add ceremony are deleted; a section whose substance has migrated elsewhere is removed, with a cross-reference where useful.

## Considered alternative

Maximal explicitness: restate everything in every document for the reader's convenience.

Discarded because co-located restatement forces drift as the corpus evolves.

The cost of a missed cross-reference is one extra click; the cost of drift is two contradictory canonical sources.

## Origin

General English: "earned" (Oxford: "deserved through one's effort") and "substance" (Oxford: "the real importance, value, or significance of something").
The commitment has cousins in YAGNI, in the KISS principle, and in the idea of load-bearing structure in architecture: only what bears load earns its place.
