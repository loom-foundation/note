Practices are right-sized to the context in which they are applied.
Rigor matches risk, ceremony matches stake, structural weight matches scale.

A practice that imposes uniform rigor across all contexts either crushes smaller adopters under unearned weight or under-serves larger adopters whose context requires more.

## Why this matters

A method that ignores context fails in two directions.
It can over-rigor the small case, so practitioners abandon it or follow it ceremonially.
It can under-rigor the large case, so it produces decisions the context will not survive.

The deeper failure is loss of trust: a right-sized practice earns compliance because the practitioner can see why the work matters.

Proportionality is not only an authoring commitment; it is a property Note provides to adopters, realized as tiered conformance against a declared profile.
The gain this principle realizes is adoptability across a spectrum of corpora, from a one-file prototype to a regulated release, each carrying only the weight its context earns.

To keep a single source of truth, the obligation and its mechanism are stated once in the right-sizing requirement and its specification, not restated here; this entry names the commitment and points at them.

## How to apply

Right-size the corpus itself.
Do not impose the heaviest available structure on every section: a technical term does not need the academic lineage a philosophical one earns; a draft does not need the records a regulated release earns.

Leave structural slots that a fuller version can grow into.

## Considered alternative

Uniform rigor: ship at full rigor and trust the adopter to apply selectively.

Discarded because adopters routinely cannot tell which parts are substance and which are ceremony appropriate to a larger context than theirs.

The discrimination burden belongs with the authors, done once and reused by every adopter.

## Origin

General English: *proportionate* (Oxford: "corresponding in size or amount to something else").

Sharpened in law and regulation (intervention no greater than necessary), in risk management (ISO 31000, treatment matched to assessed risk), in contingency theory (no one best organizational form; structure must fit context), and in the Cynefin framework (match the response to the nature of the problem).
